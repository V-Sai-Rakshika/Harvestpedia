Understanding Nutrients

Every food you eat is a collection of nutrients — each one performing a specific role in your body. Here is a clear, complete guide to what nutrients are, how they work, and where to find them in fruits and vegetables.

The Basics

What are nutrients?

Nutrients are the substances your body extracts from food to survive, grow, and function properly.

The building blocks of your health

When you eat, your digestive system breaks food down into its core components — nutrients. These are absorbed into the bloodstream and delivered to every cell in your body, fueling everything from breathing to building muscle.

Nutrients are divided into two broad categories: macronutrients, which your body needs in large amounts for energy and structure, and micronutrients, which are required in smaller amounts but are equally essential for health and wellbeing.

Fruits and vegetables are among the richest and most diverse sources of nutrients in the human diet — providing a natural combination of vitamins, minerals, fiber, and phytocompounds that work together in ways supplements cannot replicate.

Macronutrients

Protein, carbohydrates, and fats — your primary sources of energy and structural material.

Micronutrients

Vitamins and minerals that regulate body processes and protect your cells from damage.

Phytonutrients

Naturally occurring plant compounds with powerful antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects.

Water

The most essential nutrient of all — fruits and vegetables provide high-quality dietary water.

Macronutrients

Energy and structure

Macronutrients are the three major nutrient groups that provide calories and form the physical structure of your body.

Protein

Macronutrient

What it does

Protein is the foundation of every cell in your body. It builds and repairs muscle tissue, produces enzymes and hormones, and supports immune function. Proteins are made up of chains of amino acids, nine of which your body cannot produce on its own.

Why it matters

Without adequate protein, the body cannot maintain muscle mass, heal wounds, or produce the antibodies it needs to fight infection. For those following a plant-based diet, combining diverse protein sources ensures a complete amino acid profile.

Plant sources

Edamame Green Peas Lentils Broccoli Spinach Avocado

Carbohydrates

Macronutrient

What it does

Carbohydrates are your body's preferred fuel source. When digested, they break down into glucose — the primary energy currency used by your brain, muscles, and organs. Complex carbohydrates also provide fiber, which supports digestive health.

Why it matters

Not all carbohydrates behave the same way. Simple carbohydrates are digested quickly and cause rapid blood sugar spikes. Complex carbohydrates — found abundantly in whole fruits and vegetables — digest slowly, providing sustained energy and improved blood sugar control.

Plant sources

Banana Sweet Potato Mango Beetroot Plantain Apple

Fats

Macronutrient

What it does

Dietary fats support brain function, cushion organs, regulate hormones, and enable the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K. Healthy unsaturated fats — particularly omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids — are essential for cardiovascular and neurological health.

Why it matters

Fat has historically been misunderstood. The key distinction is fat quality. Natural plant-based fats from fruits like avocado and coconut provide beneficial lipids without the inflammatory effects associated with processed trans fats.

Plant sources

Avocado Coconut Olives Flaxseed Walnuts Chia Seeds
Micronutrients

Small amounts, large impact

Vitamins and minerals are required in smaller quantities than macronutrients, but their absence leads to serious health consequences.

Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds that support hundreds of biochemical reactions in your body. They are divided into two groups: fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), which are stored in body fat and the liver, and water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C), which must be replenished regularly through diet.

Fruits and vegetables are the most concentrated dietary sources of vitamins. A single serving of bell pepper, for example, exceeds the daily recommended intake of Vitamin C.

Minerals

Minerals are inorganic elements absorbed from soil into the plants we eat. They are categorized as macrominerals — needed in larger amounts, such as calcium, magnesium, and potassium — and trace minerals, needed in very small quantities, such as iron, zinc, and selenium.

Plant-based foods are excellent mineral sources, particularly leafy greens, legumes, root vegetables, and many fruits.

Key Nutrients

Nutrients worth knowing

These four nutrients are especially important for everyday health — and abundantly found in the natural foods Harvestpedia covers.

Potassium

Macromineral

Potassium is a mineral and electrolyte that regulates fluid balance, nerve signals, and muscle contractions. It plays a central role in maintaining healthy blood pressure by counteracting the effects of sodium.

A diet rich in potassium is associated with a significantly reduced risk of stroke, heart disease, and kidney stones. Most adults do not consume enough of it.

Banana Avocado Sweet Potato Spinach Coconut Water

Iron

Trace Mineral

Iron is essential for producing hemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from your lungs to every part of your body. It also supports energy metabolism and immune function.

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide, particularly among women. Plant-based (non-heme) iron is absorbed more effectively when consumed alongside Vitamin C-rich foods.

Spinach Beetroot Peas Mulberry Dried Apricot

Biotin

B-Vitamin · B7

Biotin, also known as Vitamin B7, helps your body convert carbohydrates, fats, and proteins into usable energy. It is essential for cell growth, fatty acid synthesis, and the maintenance of healthy hair, skin, and nails.

Biotin also plays a role in regulating gene expression and supporting proper embryonic growth, making it particularly important during pregnancy.

Avocado Sweet Potato Cauliflower Raspberries Onion

Fiber

Dietary Carbohydrate

Dietary fiber is the indigestible portion of plant foods. Soluble fiber dissolves in water and forms a gel that slows digestion, stabilizes blood sugar, and lowers LDL cholesterol. Insoluble fiber adds bulk to stool and promotes regular bowel movements.

Fiber also acts as a prebiotic — feeding the beneficial bacteria in your gut microbiome, which in turn influences immunity, inflammation, and even mental health.
